Entropy Dominance in Platform Competition:
A Game-Theoretic Foundation

Anonymous

Department of Economics

February 2026  ·  Preliminary — comments welcome

Abstract. The platform that wins is the one that maximally expands what is possible outside itself. We propose ecosystem entropy $H_i$—the Shannon entropy of the downstream activities, entities, and economic arrangements that platform $i$ makes viable—as the competitive primitive, and study its consequences under the payoff $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$. The theorems below are mathematical consequences of this model. Theorem 1: at equal market share, the max-entropy platform is the unique best response; network-effect terms cancel exactly, leaving the entropy gap as the sole selection criterion. Theorem 2: under Kandori–Mailath–Rob dynamics with vanishing mutation rate, the max-entropy platform is the unique long-run survivor. Theorem 3: a smaller platform defeats a larger incumbent if and only if $\Delta H > \nu\bigl((1-x_0)^\gamma - x_0^\gamma\bigr)$; when $\Delta H > \nu$, it wins from any initial share. Theorem 4: the convergence rate is $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$, additive in entropy gap and network strength. Theorem 6: platforms underinvest in openness relative to the social optimum; the welfare gap is strictly increasing in $\nu\gamma$, providing a formal basis for interoperability mandates. Proposition 2.1 proves that Metcalfe's Law, Reed's Law, and Farrell–Klemperer lock-in are each exact limiting cases of the model — recovered at $\Delta H = 0$ — establishing entropy dominance as the general theory of which these are special cases. We propose the Chao–Shen estimator and an IV regression as the methodology for testing these predictions against data.

1. Introduction

Between 2002 and 2021, four displacement events produced the same pattern. Google displaced Yahoo by making a far more diverse set of websites findable and commercially viable—expanding what the web could be, not the complexity of the search interface. Android displaced Symbian by enabling a broader set of app categories than Symbian's proprietary SDK permitted. Ethereum captured developer share from Bitcoin by making an exponentially larger set of programmable applications possible on-chain. TikTok displaced YouTube for short video by enabling a distribution of creator formats that YouTube's structure could not support. In each case the winner was not the larger incumbent; it was the platform that expanded what was possible outside itself.

Metcalfe's Law and Reed's Law predict the incumbent should always win: both model platform value solely as a function of user count $N$ and have no variable capturing the diversity of what a platform enables. Neither framework can explain the pattern above. This paper proposes that downstream ecosystem entropy is the missing variable.

The network-effects literature has two foundational claims. Rohlfs (1974) showed that demand for a communications service depends on the number of participants, establishing the theoretical basis for the $\nu x_i^\gamma$ term in the payoff function. Katz and Shapiro (1985, 1994) formalised this as a competitive externality and showed that markets with network effects can lock into suboptimal standards. Both frameworks model network value as a function of user count alone, with no variable capturing the diversity of what a platform enables. The entropy model extends these frameworks by decomposing platform value into two orthogonal components: coordination value ($\nu x_i^\gamma$, which is the Katz–Shapiro term) and ecosystem scope ($H_i$, which is new). When $\Delta H = 0$, the model collapses to Katz–Shapiro competition exactly (Proposition 2.1a–b). When $\Delta H > 0$, the new term dominates at equal market share (Theorem 1), providing the missing variable that explains the historical pattern.

Proposition 2.1 formalises the relationship between this model and the existing literature. Metcalfe's Law, Reed's Law, and Farrell–Klemperer lock-in each emerge as exact limiting cases at $\Delta H = 0$: when ecosystem entropy is equal across platforms, the model reduces to standard size-based competition. When $\Delta H > 0$, the entropy gap generates competitive pressure independent of installed-base size.

The thesis: the platform that wins is the one that maximally expands what is possible outside itself. We define ecosystem entropy $H_i$ as the Shannon entropy of the downstream activities, entities, and economic arrangements that platform $i$ makes viable—not the entropy of the platform's internal interface. For Google, $\Omega_\text{Google}$ is the set of websites made discoverable and commercially viable. For Ethereum, $\Omega_\text{ETH}$ is the set of distinct smart contract functions deployed on-chain; the Dune Analytics query that counts 4-byte function selectors measures $|\Omega_\text{ETH}|$ directly (Section 6.4). For Android, $\Omega_\text{Android}$ is the set of app categories the open SDK makes viable. The measure $\mu_i(\omega)$ records realized downstream usage; $H_i$ is its Shannon entropy.

We formalize the thesis under the payoff $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$, where $\nu x_i^\gamma$ is a standard superlinear network externality. The results that follow are mathematical consequences of this model. Theorem 1 (Risk Dominance): the max-entropy platform is the unique best response at equal market share; network-effect terms cancel exactly, leaving the entropy gap as the sole selection criterion. Theorem 2 (Stochastic Stability): under Kandori–Mailath–Rob dynamics, the max-entropy platform is the unique long-run survivor; lock-in to a low-entropy equilibrium is a measure-zero event. Theorem 3 (Incumbent Erosion): a smaller platform wins from initial share $x_0$ if and only if $\Delta H > \nu((1-x_0)^\gamma - x_0^\gamma)$; when $\Delta H > \nu$, it wins from any initial share, including startup entry. Theorem 4: the convergence rate is $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$, additive in entropy gap and network strength. Theorem 6: platforms underinvest in openness; the welfare gap grows strictly with $\nu\gamma$, with direct policy implications.

Relationship to existing work. Two-sided market theory (Rochet–Tirole 2003; Armstrong 2006; Weyl 2010) characterizes optimal pricing given a fixed platform strategy space; the entropy model adds the choice of $H_i$ as a strategic variable. Farrell and Klemperer (2007) characterize lock-in under network effects; their model is recovered here as the $\Delta H \to 0$ limit (Proposition 2.1c). Sandholm's (2010) replicator ODE is the dynamical law; the contribution is identifying ecosystem entropy as the payoff primitive that drives it. The formal apparatus is drawn from all three literatures; the claim is that downstream ecosystem entropy, rather than platform size, is the correct competitive primitive.

Policy stakes. If Theorem 6 is an accurate description of reality, it provides a formal welfare basis for interoperability mandates. The welfare gap $H^{SO} - H^{NE}$ is largest where $\nu\gamma$ is largest—precisely the markets where regulators have historically intervened. The model generates a specific, falsifiable prediction: platforms subject to interoperability mandates should exhibit downstream entropy increases proportional to their pre-mandate $\hat\nu\hat\gamma$.

Paper outline. Section 2 states the model; the Ethereum case study of Section 6.4 is the canonical measurement example. Sections 3–5 derive the main results. Section 6 develops the empirical methodology. Sections 7–10 treat two-sided platforms, platform decline, transition timing, and welfare. Sections 11–13 give falsifiable predictions, related work, and open questions.

Three complementary models. The paper uses three distinct analytical frameworks, each addressing a different question, and sharing the payoff $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$. (A) The static platform selection game (Sections 3–4): at fixed entropy levels, which platform is risk-dominant (Theorem 1) and stochastically stable under KMR mutation dynamics (Theorem 2)? (B) The replicator ODE (Sections 5–9): given fixed entropy levels, what is the deterministic path of market share, under what conditions does the entrant win (Theorem 3), at what rate (Theorem 4), and how long does transition take (Proposition 9.1)? (C) The two-stage openness game (Section 10): when entropy investment is costly, how much openness do platforms choose (Proposition 10.1), and how does the equilibrium level compare to the social optimum (Theorem 6)? Models (A) and (B) are exact consequences of the payoff specification; model (C) additionally uses the Tullock contest function as a tractable approximation to winning probabilities (Remark 10.1). Results in (C) depend on this approximation; results in (A) and (B) do not.

2. Model

2.1 Platforms and Microstates

Definition 2.1 (Platform Ecosystem). A platform ecosystem $\mathcal{E}_i$ is a pair $(\Omega_i, \mu_i)$ where $\Omega_i$ is the set of distinct downstream activities, entities, or economic arrangements that become viable by virtue of platform $i$'s existence—for Google, the websites discoverable and commercially viable via its index; for Ethereum, the deployed smart contract function selectors; for Android, the app categories enabled by the open SDK—and $\mu_i : \Omega_i \to [0,1]$ is a probability measure over realized downstream usage, $\sum_{\omega} \mu_i(\omega) = 1$.
Definition 2.2 (Ecosystem Entropy). The ecosystem entropy of platform $i$ is: $$H_i = -\sum_{\omega \in \Omega_i} \mu_i(\omega) \ln \mu_i(\omega) \;\geq\; 0.$$ Under the uniform measure over $|\Omega_i|$ microstates, $H_i = \ln |\Omega_i|$. Under a Zipf($\alpha$) distribution, $H_i = \tfrac{\alpha-1}{\alpha} \ln |\Omega_i| + O(1)$ (Lemma 6.1 below). $H_i$ is observable from platform data via the Chao–Shen estimator (Section 6). The use of Shannon entropy as the competitive primitive follows Jaynes (1957): maximising entropy yields the least-biased distribution inference from partial market information, grounding $H_i$ in information-theoretic rather than ad hoc foundations.
Remark 2.0 (Microstate Granularity). The competitive ranking of platforms by $H_i$ depends on the granularity at which $\Omega_i$ is partitioned. Splitting one microstate into two increases $H_i$ mechanically. The model therefore requires that $\Omega_i$ be defined at a consistent level of granularity across competing platforms—the same category taxonomy applied to both sides of each comparison. Three principles govern this in practice. (i) Natural granularity: developer platforms admit a canonical partition from API surface enumeration (4-byte function selectors for Ethereum, app-store category codes for mobile OS, GitHub topic tags for frameworks). Use the finest unambiguous unit that the platform itself exposes. (ii) Scope restriction: pure conduit platforms (SMS, voice telephony) lack a distinct downstream activity distribution and are outside the model's scope; the empirical methodology of Section 6 applies only where $\Omega_i$ is enumerable from platform data. (iii) Matched comparison: entropy comparisons across platforms in the same market (Android vs. iOS, Ethereum vs. Bitcoin) use the same topic taxonomy (Section 6.5) so that $\Delta H = H_1 - H_2$ is granularity-invariant within the comparison.

2.2 Agent Payoffs

There is a continuum of agents $j \in [0,1]$. The state is $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \Delta^{n-1}$, where $x_i$ is the fraction of agents on platform $i$. Agent $j$'s payoff from choosing platform $i$ is:

Payoff Function $$u_i(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \underbrace{H_i}_{\text{entropy value}} \;+\; \underbrace{\nu \cdot x_i^\gamma}_{\text{network effects}}, \qquad \nu > 0,\; \gamma \geq 1.$$

The $H_i$ term represents the proposed primitive: agents value the richness of what a platform makes possible outside itself. A platform that enables more diverse downstream activities offers more distinct productive possibilities, and the model assumes agents prefer richer downstream ecosystems. Whether agents actually respond to ecosystem entropy—rather than to platform size, brand, or switching cost alone—is the central empirical question. The $\nu x_i^\gamma$ term is a standard superlinear coordination externality: more co-participants raises payoff through interaction value. Both components are estimable from data in principle: $H_i$ via the Chao–Shen estimator (Section 6.1); $\nu$ and $\gamma$ via the IV regression of Section 6.2.

Remark 2.1 (Micro-foundation of the $H_i$ term). The entropy term has two complementary micro-foundations. The first, from information-value theory (Bernardo, 1979): an agent joining platform $i$ benefits from the productive possibilities the platform makes accessible in the world: if interacting with downstream activity $\omega \sim \mu_i$ yields utility $-\ln\mu_i(\omega)$—rarer, more unexpected activities deliver higher creative or productive value than common ones—then expected utility is exactly $\mathbb{E}_{\mu_i}[-\ln\mu_i(\omega)] = H_i$, and the linearity of $u_i$ in $H_i$ follows from this expectation rather than from a separate assumption. A developer joining Ethereum, for instance, benefits from the diversity of applications Ethereum makes possible to interact with—the realized downstream distribution $\mu_\text{ETH}$—not merely from the complexity of Ethereum's internal interface. The second foundation comes from rational inattention (Matejka & McKay, 2015): agents with a Shannon mutual information processing cost $\lambda$ optimally choose platform $i$ with probability $\propto \exp(H_i/\lambda)$; as $\lambda \to 0$, this selects the max-entropy platform with probability 1, recovering the same payoff structure from a modern decision-theoretic foundation without the surprisal-utility assumption. The two micro-foundations agree on the ordinal ranking of platforms by $H_i$ but differ in the interpetation: the Bernardo foundation emphasizes productive value of diversity; the Matejka–McKay foundation emphasizes bounded rationality under information constraints.
Remark 2.2 (Scope and the Central Assumption). Agents respond to $H_i$: they choose platforms based partly on what the platform makes possible outside itself. This assumption is most plausible when the downstream ecosystem is directly observable (developer platforms with public API catalogs, blockchains with on-chain ABI selectors, app stores with category counts) and when the platform's choices causally determine what can be built. It is least plausible for pure conduit platforms where value is reach rather than generative diversity. Whether the assumption holds—and in which markets—is what the empirical methodology of Section 6 is designed to test.

2.3 The Platform Selection Game

Definition 2.3 (Platform Selection Game). The platform selection game $\Gamma = (\mathcal{J}, \mathcal{P}, \{u_i\})$ has:
• Players: continuum $\mathcal{J} = [0,1]$.
• Actions: $\mathcal{P} = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ (choose one platform).
• Payoffs: $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$ as above.

2.4 Existing Theories as Limiting Cases

The payoff $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$ nests the existing network-competition literature as special cases parameterised by $\Delta H$. At $\Delta H = 0$, competition reduces to pure network-effects. At $\Delta H > 0$, the entropy gap operates alongside the network externality. At $\Delta H > \nu$, entropy dominates: the entrant wins from any initial share regardless of size. Proposition 2.1 states these transitions formally.

Proposition 2.1 (Recovery of Existing Theories). Let $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, 1-x_1)$ with $x_1 \equiv x$. The payoff $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$ recovers the following as limiting cases:

(a) Metcalfe's Law. Suppose $H_1 = H_2$ and $\gamma = 2$. Then $u_1 - u_2 = \nu(x^2 - (1-x)^2) = \nu(x - (1-x))(x + (1-x)) = \nu(2x-1)$ (using $x_1 + x_2 = 1$). The replicator ODE is $\dot{x} = x(1-x)\cdot \nu(2x-1)$, with interior fixed point at $x^* = 1/2$. The platform with initial share $x_0 > 1/2$ wins. $\square$

(b) Winner-take-all at majority (Reed regime). Suppose $H_1 = H_2$ and $\gamma \to \infty$. For $x_1 > 1/2$: $$\frac{x_1^\gamma}{(1-x_1)^\gamma} = \left(\frac{x_1}{1-x_1}\right)^\gamma \to \infty$$ as $\gamma \to \infty$, so $u_1 - u_2 = \nu(x_1^\gamma - (1-x_1)^\gamma) \to +\infty$. Any majority share is decisive; the separatrix collapses to $x^* \to 1/2^-$. $\square$

(c) Farrell–Klemperer lock-in. Suppose $\Delta H \to 0^+$. From Theorem 3, the separatrix $x^*$ solves $\Delta H = \nu\,g(x^*)$ where $g(x) = (1-x)^\gamma - x^\gamma$. Since $g(1/2) = 0$ and $g$ is strictly decreasing on $(0,1/2)$, we have $x^* \to 1/2$ as $\Delta H \to 0^+$. The incumbent's basin of attraction $\{x_0 > x^*\} \to \{x_0 > 1/2\}$: majority share becomes necessary and sufficient for retention. $\square$

(d) Pure ecosystem competition. Suppose $\nu \to 0$. The replicator reduces to $\dot{x} = x(1-x)\,\Delta H > 0$ for all $x_0 \in (0,1)$. No interior separatrix exists; the max-entropy platform wins from any initial share regardless of size. $\square$

Remark 2.3 (The role of $\Delta H$ as the generalization parameter). $\Delta H$ plays the structural role of spacetime curvature in the relationship between General Relativity and Newtonian mechanics: at $\Delta H = 0$, the model is exactly the existing theory; at $\Delta H > 0$, new competitive forces emerge. The historical displacement events of Section 1 — Google, Android, Ethereum, TikTok — are precisely the markets where $\Delta H > 0$ was large relative to $\nu$, placing them outside the range of validity of Metcalfe's and Reed's Laws. 3. Static Equilibrium Analysis

3.1 Nash Equilibria

Every corner $\mathbf{e}_i$ (all agents on platform $i$) is a Nash equilibrium: any single deviating agent earns $H_j + \nu \cdot \varepsilon^\gamma \to H_j$ as $\varepsilon \to 0^+$, which is strictly less than $H_i + \nu$ for any $\nu > 0$. Interior Nash equilibria also exist (agents mix such that all active platforms give equal payoff), but they are generically unstable and serve only as basin boundaries in the dynamical analysis of Section 5.

3.2 Risk Dominance

The key observation is that at equal market share, the network-effect term $\nu(1/2)^\gamma$ is identical for both platforms, so it cancels from the payoff comparison. Entropy alone determines which platform is the better response.

Theorem 1 (Risk Dominance). In any 2-platform subgame of $\Gamma$, the platform $i^* = \arg\max_i H_i$ is risk-dominant: it is the unique best response at equal market share $\mathbf{x} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{e}_{i^*} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{e}_j$ for every $j \neq i^*$.
Proof. At $x_{i^*} = x_j = 1/2$: $$u_{i^*}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) - u_j\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \bigl(H_{i^*} + \nu(1/2)^\gamma\bigr) - \bigl(H_j + \nu(1/2)^\gamma\bigr) = H_{i^*} - H_j > 0.$$ The network-effect terms $\nu(1/2)^\gamma$ cancel exactly. Risk dominance follows from the standard definition (Harsanyi & Selten 1988): $i^*$ is the unique best response at the uniform mixture, so it satisfies the risk-dominance criterion strictly. $\square$
Remark 3.1 (Why this requires no conditions). Theorem 1 holds for all $\nu > 0$ and $\gamma \geq 1$, unconditionally. The network terms cancel at equal market share by symmetry. The entropy gap $H_{i^*} - H_j > 0$ is the sole determinant of risk dominance. Larger network effects ($\nu$) accelerate convergence speed (Theorem 4) but do not change which platform is risk-dominant.

4. Stochastic Stability

Risk dominance establishes which platform is the unique static best response, but does not determine long-run outcomes under dynamic adjustment. Stochastic stability under Kandori–Mailath–Rob (KMR 1993) dynamics closes this gap.

Because the max-entropy platform is risk-dominant (Theorem 1), its basin of attraction is strictly larger than that of any competitor, so it requires more mutations to escape. This size asymmetry is the escape-resistance gap that drives long-run selection.

Theorem 2 (Unique Stochastically Stable Equilibrium). Consider the discrete population version of $\Gamma$ with $N$ agents. Under KMR best-response dynamics with mutation rate $\varepsilon \in (0,1)$: each period, one agent is selected uniformly at random; with probability $1-\varepsilon$ they best-respond to the current state; with probability $\varepsilon$ they choose each platform with equal probability. As $\varepsilon \to 0$, the unique stochastically stable state is $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$, the state in which all agents are on the max-entropy platform $i^* = \arg\max_i H_i$.
Proof. By KMR (1993, Theorem 2), the stochastically stable states in a 2-strategy coordination game are those with the highest escape resistance: the minimum number of mutations required to exit their basin of attraction under best-response dynamics.

Let $x^* \in (0, 1/2)$ be the unique unstable interior equilibrium (the separatrix, derived in Section 5). The basin of attraction of $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ under best-response dynamics is $\{k : k/N > x^*\}$ and that of $\mathbf{e}_j$ is $\{k : k/N < x^*\}$.

The resistance of $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ is the minimum mutations to move from $k = N$ to the basin of $\mathbf{e}_j$, i.e., to reach $k \leq Nx^*$: this costs $N - \lfloor Nx^* \rfloor \approx N(1-x^*)$ mutations. The resistance of $\mathbf{e}_j$ is approximately $Nx^*$ mutations (from $k=0$ to above $Nx^*$). Since $x^* < 1/2$ by Theorem 1 (risk dominance), $1 - x^* > 1/2 > x^*$, so $N(1-x^*) > Nx^*$: $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ has strictly greater resistance. By KMR Theorem 2, $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ is the unique stochastically stable equilibrium. $\square$
Remark 4.2 (Stochastic Potential and Escape Resistance). The stochastic potential framework of Young (1993) and Blume (1993) makes the selection mechanism transparent. Define the resistance of escaping state $\mathbf{e}_i$ as the minimum number of mutations needed to leave its basin of attraction. The stochastically stable states maximize this resistance. In our two-platform game, the resistance of $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ is $R_{i^*} = N(1 - x^*)$ (mutations to drop from $k = N$ to $k \leq Nx^*$), while the resistance of $\mathbf{e}_j$ is $R_j = Nx^*$. Since $x^* < 1/2$ by Theorem 1, $R_{i^*} > R_j$: the max-entropy platform has strictly greater escape resistance. Young's (1993) stochastic potential criterion then selects $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ uniquely. The proof makes explicit that risk dominance (Theorem 1) generates the basin asymmetry that drives stochastic selection, and that this holds for any mutation rate $\varepsilon \to 0$, not just for KMR-uniform mutations (Foster & Young 1990).
Remark 4.1 (Interpretation). Theorem 2 makes a strong long-run prediction: regardless of history, coordination failures, or early incumbent advantage, the max-entropy platform is selected in the long run whenever mutations are small. Theorem 3 characterizes the finite-time path under deterministic replicator dynamics.

5. Replicator Dynamics

5.1 The Replicator ODE

The replicator ODE is adopted as the dynamic law for three reasons. First, it is the unique dynamics satisfying payoff-monotonicity (platforms with above-average payoff grow in share) and the Folk Theorem of evolutionary game theory: Nash equilibria are the only rest points, and strictly dominated strategies are eliminated (Weibull 1995, Ch. 3; Sandholm 2010, Thm. 5.4). Second, it provides the cleanest analytical characterisation of the separatrix $x^*$ and convergence rate $\lambda$; alternative dynamics (best-response, logit, Brown–von Neumann–Nash) produce qualitatively similar corners and interior equilibria but less tractable transition expressions. Third, in platform markets with large populations of heterogeneous adopters — the empirical settings of Section 6 — aggregate share dynamics are well approximated by population-level ODEs even when individual switching is lumpy, by a law-of-large-numbers argument (see Sandholm 2010, Ch. 10). Results that depend only on the existence and location of $x^*$ (Theorems 2 and 3) are robust across all payoff-monotone dynamics; results that depend on the specific rate $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$ (Theorem 4, Proposition 9.1) are specific to the replicator and should be interpreted as approximations in markets where switching is discrete.

Under the standard replicator dynamics—agents switch to platforms with above-average payoff at a rate proportional to the payoff difference—the population state evolves as:

$$\dot{x}_i \;=\; x_i\bigl(u_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{u}(\mathbf{x})\bigr), \qquad \bar{u}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_j x_j u_j(\mathbf{x}).$$

With $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$, this becomes:

$$\dot{x}_i = x_i\Bigl(H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma - \bar{H} - \nu\bar{X}^\gamma\Bigr),$$

where $\bar{H} = \sum_j x_j H_j$ is the mean entropy and $\bar{X}^\gamma = \sum_j x_j^{1+\gamma}$ is the mean network-effect term. For two platforms, with $x_2 = 1 - x_1$:

$$\boxed{\;\dot{x}_1 = x_1(1-x_1)\Bigl(\Delta H + \nu\bigl(x_1^\gamma - (1-x_1)^\gamma\bigr)\Bigr)\;}$$

where $\Delta H = H_1 - H_2 > 0$. This ODE is the standard replicator equation (Taylor & Jonker 1978) applied to the payoff $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$; no additional assumptions are made beyond payoff-proportional switching. Hofbauer and Sigmund (1998, ch. 7) provide the complete convergence and stability analysis for this class of ODE.

5.2 Interior Equilibria and the Separatrix

Interior fixed points satisfy $\dot{x}_1 = 0$ with $x_1 \in (0,1)$, which requires $\Delta H + \nu(x_1^\gamma - (1-x_1)^\gamma) = 0$, i.e.:

$$\Delta H = \nu\bigl((1-x^*)^\gamma - (x^*)^\gamma\bigr).$$

Let $g(x) = (1-x)^\gamma - x^\gamma$. Since $g'(x) = -\gamma[(1-x)^{\gamma-1} + x^{\gamma-1}] < 0$, $g$ is strictly decreasing on $(0,1)$. Thus there is a unique solution $x^* \in (0, 1/2)$ for any $\Delta H \in (0, \nu)$. If $\Delta H \geq \nu$ (i.e., $\Delta H \geq \nu \cdot g(0) = \nu$), there is no interior equilibrium and platform 1 wins from any initial condition.

For specific values of $\gamma$:

  • $\gamma = 1$: $g(x) = 1-2x$, so $x^* = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\Delta H}{2\nu}$.
  • $\gamma = 2$: $g(x) = (1-x)^2 - x^2 = 1-2x$, so $x^* = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\Delta H}{2\nu}$ (same).
  • $\gamma \geq 3$: $x^*$ is solved numerically; the basin of attraction of $\mathbf{e}_1$ grows relative to $\gamma = 1$.

5.3 Incumbent Erosion Condition

A platform's entropy advantage must overcome the incumbent's network-effect buffer, which is proportional to the share asymmetry $(1-x_0)^\gamma - x_0^\gamma$. When the entropy gap exceeds the maximum possible buffer ($\Delta H > \nu$), the entrant wins from any starting position.

Theorem 3 (Incumbent Erosion Condition). In the 2-platform replicator with payoffs $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$, the following are equivalent for any starting point $x_0 \in (0,1)$:

(1) The replicator converges to $\mathbf{e}_1$ (platform 1 wins).
(2) $x_0 > x^*$, where $x^*$ is the unique root of $\Delta H = \nu\bigl((1-x)^\gamma - x^\gamma\bigr)$ in $(0, 1/2)$.
(3) $\Delta H > \nu\bigl((1-x_0)^\gamma - x_0^\gamma\bigr)$.

Corollary: If $\Delta H > \nu$, platform 1 wins from any initial share $x_0 > 0$—including entering the market as a startup. If $\Delta H > 0$ but $\Delta H \leq \nu$, platform 1 wins if and only if its initial share exceeds $x^* = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\Delta H}{2\nu}$ (for $\gamma \leq 2$).
Proof. (2 $\Leftrightarrow$ 3): Since $g(x) = (1-x)^\gamma - x^\gamma$ is strictly decreasing, $x_0 > x^*$ iff $g(x_0) < g(x^*) = \Delta H/\nu$, which is (3).

(1 $\Leftrightarrow$ 2): The ODE $\dot{x}_1 = x_1(1-x_1)(\Delta H + \nu(x_1^\gamma - (1-x_1)^\gamma))$ has $\text{sgn}(\dot{x}_1) = \text{sgn}(\Delta H + \nu(x_1^\gamma - (1-x_1)^\gamma))$ for $x_1 \in (0,1)$. This expression is zero at $x^*$, positive for $x_1 > x^*$, and negative for $x_1 < x^*$. Therefore $x^*$ is the unique unstable fixed point separating the basins of $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$, and trajectories flow to $\mathbf{e}_1$ iff $x_0 > x^*$.

Corollary: $g(0^+) = (1-0)^\gamma - 0 = 1$, so $\Delta H > \nu \cdot g(x_0)$ holds for all $x_0 > 0$ whenever $\Delta H > \nu$, making the basin of $\mathbf{e}_1$ the entire interior $(0, 1)$. $\square$

5.4 Convergence Rate

Near the winning corner, both the entropy advantage and the network effect push stragglers toward the dominant platform independently. Their contributions to convergence speed add, without interaction.

Theorem 4 (Convergence Rate). Near the attracting corner $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$, let $\varepsilon = 1 - x_{i^*}$ be small. The linearized dynamics are: $$\dot{\varepsilon} = -(\Delta H + \nu)\,\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2).$$ Hence $\varepsilon(t) = O\bigl(e^{-(\Delta H + \nu)(t - t_0)}\bigr)$. The convergence rate is $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$: both entropy advantage and network effects independently accelerate convergence, and the rate is linear in each.
Proof. With $x_1 = 1-\varepsilon$, $x_2 = \varepsilon$: \begin{align*} u_2 - \bar{u} &= (1-x_2)(u_2 - u_1) = (1-\varepsilon)\bigl(-\Delta H + \nu(\varepsilon^\gamma - (1-\varepsilon)^\gamma)\bigr). \end{align*} For small $\varepsilon$: $\varepsilon^\gamma \to 0$ (since $\gamma \geq 1$) and $(1-\varepsilon)^\gamma = 1 - \gamma\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2)$, so: $$u_2 - \bar{u} = (1-\varepsilon)\bigl(-\Delta H - \nu + \nu\gamma\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2)\bigr) = -(\Delta H + \nu) + O(\varepsilon).$$ Then $\dot\varepsilon = \dot{x}_2 = x_2(u_2 - \bar{u}) = \varepsilon \cdot (-(\Delta H + \nu) + O(\varepsilon)) = -(\Delta H + \nu)\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2)$. $\square$
Remark 5.1 (Multi-Platform Rate). For $n$ platforms, linearizing near $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ gives $\dot\varepsilon_j \approx -(H_{i^*} - H_j + \nu)\varepsilon_j$ for each competitor $j$. The effective rate is $\lambda^* = \min_{j \neq i^*}(H_{i^*} - H_j) + \nu$: the bottleneck is the competitor with the smallest entropy gap.
Remark 5.2 (Potential Game at $\nu = 0$; Coordination Game at $\nu > 0$). When $\nu = 0$, the payoff function $u_i(\mathbf{x}) = H_i$ does not depend on the population state, so $\Gamma$ is a potential game (Sandholm 2010, Definition 6.3) with potential $\Phi(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i x_i H_i$. The replicator converges globally to the potential maximizer $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ by Sandholm's Theorem 6.6 and Hofbauer & Sigmund (1998, ch. 8).

For $\nu > 0$, the coordination externality $\nu x_i^\gamma$ breaks potential structure: no potential function exists because payoffs are not symmetric in $x_i$ and $x_j$. This forces the analysis to stochastic stability (Theorem 2) rather than Lyapunov arguments. The loss of potential structure explains why the finite-time prediction (Theorem 3) requires the separatrix condition rather than a simple entropy comparison, and why long-run stability requires mutation to escape locally stable sub-optimal corners. The welfare implications of Theorem 6 follow from the fact that agents do not internalise the coordination externality ($\nu$ creates a positive congestion effect that each agent ignores), which is a standard feature of congestion games.
Theorem 7 (Three-Phase Platform Dynamics). For an entrant platform 1 with $\Delta H > 0$, $\nu > 0$, $\gamma \geq 1$, and initial share $x_0 \in (0,1)$, define three phases:
  • Phase I (Contraction). $x_0 \in (0, x^*)$. The replicator satisfies $\dot x_1 < 0$ for all $t \geq 0$; the entrant's share converges monotonically to 0. Organic growth is impossible without external support. The minimum viable boost — the smallest exogenous share increment that exits Phase I — is $\delta_{\min} = x^* - x_0$, where for $\gamma \leq 2$ the separatrix has the closed form $x^* = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\Delta H}{2\nu}$, giving: $$\delta_{\min} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\Delta H}{2\nu} - x_0 \qquad (\gamma \leq 2).$$ For $\gamma > 2$, $x^*$ must be solved numerically from $\Delta H = \nu\bigl((1-x^*)^\gamma - (x^*)^\gamma\bigr)$ (Section 5.2), and $\delta_{\min} = x^* - x_0$ accordingly; the Phase I/III partition is still exact.
  • Phase II (Critical Transition). $x_0 = x^*$, the unstable interior equilibrium. Any perturbation $\delta > 0$ above $x^*$ initiates Phase III; any $\delta < 0$ returns to Phase I.
  • Phase III (Escape Velocity). $x_0 \in (x^*, 1)$. The replicator satisfies $\dot x_1 > 0$ and the entrant converges to $\mathbf{e}_1$ at exponential rate $$\lambda \;=\; \Delta H + \nu + O(\varepsilon),$$ where $\varepsilon = 1 - x_1 \to 0$ (Theorem 4). Growth is self-sustaining: no further external support is required.
Special case. When $\Delta H > \nu$, $x^* = 0$ and Phase I is empty: the entrant is in Phase III from any positive initial share (Theorem 3 Corollary).
Proof. Phase I: Theorem 3 gives $\dot x_1 < 0$ iff $x_0 < x^*$. Phase II: $x^*$ is the unique unstable interior equilibrium (Section 5.2). Phase III: Theorem 3 gives $\dot x_1 > 0$ iff $x_0 > x^*$; Theorem 4 gives the exponential convergence rate at the attracting corner $\mathbf{e}_1$. The $\delta_{\min}$ formula uses $x^* = 1/2 - \Delta H/(2\nu)$ for $\gamma \leq 2$ (Section 5.2). $\square$
Remark 7.1 (Phase Transitions Require Coordination Shocks). Under the deterministic replicator, Phase I $\to$ Phase III requires an exogenous shock moving $x_1$ above $x^*$ discontinuously (external funding, viral adoption, regulatory mandate, or promotional pricing). Under KMR stochastic dynamics, the transition can also occur via accumulated mutations, but the expected waiting time is $O(e^{N \cdot N(x^*-x_0)})$ — exponentially long in population size $N$. For large markets, the deterministic characterisation is the operative one: crossing $x^*$ requires a coordination shock, not gradual organic growth.
Proposition 6.1 (Rational Incumbent Passivity). Note: this proposition is stated here, in Section 5, because it characterises Phase I behaviour under the replicator ODE; it is numbered 6.1 because Part (ii) appeals to the openness game of Section 10.   2. Optimal entropy is unchanged (under the openness game of Section 10). The incumbent's equilibrium openness $H_2^{NE} = \sqrt{rT/(4k)}$ (Proposition 10.1) is independent of $\Delta H$: the Tullock FOC $rT/(4H_2^2) = k$ does not involve $H_1$ or $x_1$. Within the replicator model of Section 5, the analogous statement is that when $x_1 < x^*$ the replicator dynamics are independent of $H_2$ (only the separatrix $x^*$ depends on $\Delta H$), so the incumbent faces no payoff incentive to respond.
  3. Disruption trigger. The competitive threat materialises only when an exogenous shock moves $x_1$ above $x^*$. Thereafter, the replicator drives $x_1 \to 1$ at rate $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$ (Theorem 3); the incumbent's subsequent entropy investment reduces $\Delta H$ at cost $(k/2)\Delta H_2^2$ but cannot prevent convergence to $\mathbf{e}_1$ if $x_1$ already exceeds the new $x^*$.
Corollary. The rational threat threshold is $x_1 \uparrow x^*$, not $x_1 > 0$. An incumbent with $x_1 \ll x^*$ rationally allocates zero defensive resources to entropy expansion.
Proof. (i) Theorem 3: $x_0 < x^* \Rightarrow x(t) \to 0$ under the deterministic replicator, for all $H_1, H_2 > 0$. Remark 6.1 (Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation). Proposition 6.1 induces a formal taxonomy. An entrant's innovation is entropic if it generates $\Delta H > 0$ from small $x_0 \to 0$. The incumbent's dominant short-run strategy is therefore sustaining response, even though it does not eliminate the structural vulnerability created by $\Delta H > 0$.

6. Entropy Measurement

6.1 Zipf Correction

Downstream usage on real platforms is heavy-tailed: a few activity types account for the bulk of realized interactions. Lemma 6.1 shows that under Zipf scaling, entropy grows as a fraction $(\alpha-1)/\alpha$ of $\ln M$, so the entropy gap $\Delta H$ is proportional to the log ratio of ecosystem sizes and Theorem 3 applies directly.

Lemma 6.1 (Zipf Correction). Suppose usage follows a Zipf($\alpha$) distribution over $M = |\Omega_i|$ microstates, so $\mu_i(\omega_k) \propto k^{-\alpha}$ for $\alpha > 1$. Then: $$H_i = \frac{\alpha-1}{\alpha}\ln M + O(1) \quad \text{as } M \to \infty.$$ Consequently, $\Delta H = H_1 - H_2 = \tfrac{\alpha-1}{\alpha}(\ln |\Omega_1| - \ln |\Omega_2|) + O(1)$ and Theorem 3 applies with the Zipf-corrected entropy values.
Proof. The normalizing constant $Z_M = \sum_{k=1}^M k^{-\alpha} \sim M^{1-\alpha}/(\alpha-1)$ by integral approximation. With $p_k = k^{-\alpha}/Z_M$: $$H = \ln Z_M + \frac{\alpha}{Z_M}\sum_{k=1}^M k^{-\alpha}\ln k.$$ Integration by parts gives $\sum_{k=1}^M k^{-\alpha}\ln k \sim M^{1-\alpha}\ln M/(\alpha-1)$. Substituting $\ln Z_M = (1-\alpha)\ln M + O(1)$ yields $H = \tfrac{\alpha-1}{\alpha}\ln M + O(1)$. $\square$

6.2 Chao–Shen Entropy Estimator

The naive plug-in estimator $\hat{H}_\text{PI} = -\sum_i \hat{p}_i \ln \hat{p}_i$ is severely downward-biased when $|\Omega_i|$ is large relative to sample size (many microstates go unobserved). This matters: underestimating $H_1$ and $H_2$ by different amounts biases $\widehat{\Delta H}$ and can reverse the prediction of Theorem 3. We use the Chao–Shen (2003) estimator:

$$\hat{H}_{\mathrm{CS}} = -\sum_{i \in \Omega_{\mathrm{obs}}} \frac{\hat{p}_i \ln \hat{p}_i}{1 - (1 - \hat{p}_i)^n},$$

where $\hat{p}_i = n_i/n$ and the denominator is the Horvitz–Thompson inclusion probability for microstate $i$ in a sample of size $n$. This estimator is consistent for large alphabets and accounts for coverage $\hat{C} = 1 - f_1/n$ ($f_1$ = number of singletons).

Bias bounds and robustness. The Chao–Shen estimator reduces but does not eliminate finite-sample bias. Paninski (2003) establishes that for a discretised alphabet of $k$ observed types and sample size $n$, the bias of any entropy estimator scales as $O(k \ln k / n^2)$. For platform comparisons at small $n$, report bias-corrected confidence intervals $\hat{H}_\text{CS} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\hat\sigma_\text{CS}$ where $\hat\sigma_\text{CS}$ is the bootstrap standard deviation. As a robustness check, compare the Chao–Shen estimate against the Good–Turing smoothed estimator (Good 1953), which models unobserved alternatives via the frequency-of-frequencies distribution $\{f_r\}$ where $f_r$ is the count of types appearing exactly $r$ times. If the two estimates diverge by more than $5\%$, this signals that the sample-coverage assumption is violated and that $\widehat{\Delta H}$ should be reported as a range rather than a point estimate.

6.3 Estimating $\nu$ and $\gamma$

From the payoff $u_i = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$, the IV regression specification is:

$$\ln x_{i,t+1} - \ln x_{i,t} \;=\; \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \hat{H}_{i,t} + \alpha_2 \ln x_{i,t} + \varepsilon_{i,t},$$

which identifies $\gamma$ and $\nu$ via $\alpha_2/\alpha_1 = \gamma$ and $\alpha_1 \propto \nu$. The instrument for $\hat{H}$ is any exogenous event that shifts the platform's accessible microstate space without directly affecting market share: API deprecations, regulatory restrictions on data access, or major protocol upgrades. The instrument for $x_{i,t}$ is a jurisdiction-specific regulatory event (ban or license requirement) that shifts $N$ without directly altering $H$. Standard heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors apply.

Instrument validity checks. The exclusion restriction requires that API deprecations and regulatory events shift $H_i$ without directly affecting market share through channels other than ecosystem entropy. Four testable checks: (i) Timing: verify that deprecation announcements precede market-share shifts by at least 90 days, ruling out reverse causation. (ii) Parallel trends: confirm pre-treatment market-share trends are parallel across treated and control platforms. (iii) Placebo: measure $\hat H$ response to fictitious “ghost” deprecations on APIs that never existed; a spurious response indicates measurement error. (iv) Sector heterogeneity: verify that API deprecations have no significant effect on $H_i$ for non-developer platforms (retail, ride-hailing), confirming that the instrument only works when developer ecosystem breadth is economically relevant (Boudreau 2010; Seamans & Zhu 2014).

The primary quantitative test of Theorem 3 measures $\hat{H}_\text{ETH}(t)$ from on-chain ABI diversity. 7. Two-Sided Platforms and the Apple Paradox

Apple's App Store imposes curation constraints on developers and retains dominant share in premium mobile computing. The two-sided entropy decomposition resolves this directly. Apple restricts consumer-side entropy $H_C$ through App Review, simultaneously raising conditional developer entropy $H_{D \mid C}$: for any given consumer segment, a narrower but higher-quality set of developer offerings is available. Total entropy $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D \mid C}$ rises when $|\partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C| > 1$ (Proposition 7.1), giving Apple's curation strategy a formal entropy-theoretic justification.

The two-sided structure raises a classic chicken-and-egg problem (Caillaud & Jullien 2003; Rysman 2009): the consumer side must be large enough to attract developers, who in turn attract consumers. In the entropy decomposition, this sequentiality is encoded in the chain rule $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D \mid C}$: consumer entropy $H_C$ is a prerequisite for conditional developer entropy $H_{D \mid C}$, because developers can serve only the consumer types the platform has already attracted. Caillaud and Jullien's analysis of multi-homing equilibria translates directly: when consumers multi-home, $H_C$ is split across platforms, reducing each platform's $H_{D \mid C}$ and forcing differentiation on the developer side. The Apple App Review process is one mechanism for building $H_{D \mid C}$ by restricting consumer multi-homing (locking $H_C$ to Apple users only), consistent with Rysman's (2009) characterisation of bottleneck platforms.

7.1 Correct Two-Sided Decomposition

Let $C$ be the consumer microstate and $D$ the developer microstate. The correct chain-rule decomposition is:

$$H(C, D) \;=\; H_C + H_{D \mid C}.$$

$H_C$ is the entropy of consumer experience (how diverse are users' interaction patterns?). $H_{D \mid C}$ is the conditional entropy of developer diversity given the consumer type (given a consumer's needs, how many distinct developer offerings exist to meet them?).

Proposition 7.1 (Constraint-Conditional Entropy Tradeoff). A platform that restricts consumer entropy $H_C$ (curation/walled garden) can simultaneously increase $H_{D \mid C}$ (developer diversity conditional on each consumer type), raising total entropy $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D \mid C}$ if and only if: $$\frac{\partial H_{D \mid C}}{\partial H_C} < -1.$$ When this holds, the optimal platform constraint is interior: the platform imposes restrictions on the consumer side to raise the value of developer access to specific consumer segments.
Proof. Total entropy $H = H_C + H_{D \mid C}$. Differentiating with respect to $H_C$: $dH/dH_C = 1 + \partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C$. This is negative (tighter curation raises total entropy) iff $\partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C < -1$: the marginal gain in conditional developer entropy from tighter consumer curation outweighs the marginal loss in consumer entropy. $\square$

Application to Apple: Apple's App Review process reduces $H_C$ (fewer bad apps means less consumer diversity, but also less noise). It increases $H_{D \mid C}$: for any given consumer need, more high-quality, complementary developer offerings are available, because low-quality entries have been excluded and remaining developers can find their exact target segment. When $|\partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C| > 1$, the App Store's curation raises total entropy $H(C,D)$ and the model predicts Apple's dominance in the premium segment. This is not a scope-condition exception; it is the model's prediction under the correct entropy decomposition.

Remark 7.1 (Plausibility of the Curation Condition). The condition $\partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C < -1$ in Proposition 7.1 is an empirical claim, not a theorem. It requires that a marginal tightening of consumer curation raises conditional developer diversity by more than it costs in consumer-side entropy. This is plausible when consumer heterogeneity is high and developers exhibit niche complementarity: specialisation into a narrower consumer segment reveals a larger developer opportunity, attracting more distinct entrants. It is implausible when consumer preferences are homogeneous (curation reduces both $H_C$ and $H_{D \mid C}$ proportionally). A sufficient parametric condition: if $H_{D \mid C} = \alpha / H_C$ for $\alpha > 0$, then $\partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C = -\alpha/H_C^2 < -1$ iff $H_C < \sqrt{\alpha}$—the dominant platform with large consumer base ($H_C$ high) violates the condition; a niche platform with $H_C$ small satisfies it. For Apple, the condition's plausibility rests on the empirical observation that App Review curation preceded growth in app-category diversity in the premium segment; it is a research proposition to be tested, not a consequence of the information-theoretic structure.
Remark 7.2 (Correction to Prior Drafts). Earlier versions of this paper used the decomposition $H = H_C + H_D + I(C;D) + H_\text{platform}$. This is mathematically invalid: substituting $I(C;D) = H_C + H_D - H(C,D)$ gives $H = 2H_C + 2H_D - H(C,D) + H_\text{platform}$, which is nonsensical. The correct identity is $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D \mid C}$, used exclusively in this version.

8. Platform Decline: Entropy Overshoot

Theorem 3 assumes agents value ecosystem entropy monotonically. Heterogeneous complexity tolerance yields a richer dynamic: platforms can overshoot the population's median tolerance and suffer competitive decline by the same mechanism that drives growth in Theorem 3, only in reverse.

Definition 8.1 (Heterogeneous Tolerance). Agent $j$ has complexity tolerance $\theta_j \sim F(\theta)$ with density $f(\theta) > 0$. Payoff: $$u_j(i; \mathbf{x}) = \phi(H_i, \theta_j) + \nu x_i^\gamma,$$ where $\phi$ is strictly increasing in $H_i$ for $H_i < \theta_j$ and strictly decreasing for $H_i > \theta_j$, with $\phi(H_i, \theta_j)|_{H_i = \theta_j}$ being the maximum.
Theorem 5 (Entropy Overshoot and Decline). Let $\bar\theta$ be the median tolerance. Suppose $H_i(t^\dagger) = \bar\theta$. Then:

(1) For $t > t^\dagger$, the majority of agents have $\partial u_j/\partial H_i < 0$: net outflow exceeds inflow when $-\int_0^{H_i} f(\theta)\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial H_i}\bigl|_-\,d\theta > \nu x_i^\gamma$.
(2) Under the replicator, $\dot{x}_i < 0$ when the above holds: platform $i$'s share declines by the same mechanism as Theorem 3 in reverse.
(3) A competing platform $j$ with $H_j < \bar\theta$ satisfies $\partial u_j/\partial H_j > 0$ for the majority; it gains share and Theorem 3 applies to platform $j$.
Proof. Part (1): for $H_i > \bar\theta$, agents with $\theta_j < H_i$ have $\partial\phi/\partial H_i < 0$; this is a strict majority when $H_i > \bar\theta$. Net outflow dominates when the aggregate disutility exceeds the network retention benefit $\nu x_i^\gamma$, which occurs at finite $H_i > \bar\theta$ for sufficiently large $H_i - \bar\theta$ by continuity of $\phi$. (This existence argument is qualitative; Lemma 8.1 below supplies an explicit closed-form threshold under the uniform-tolerance, quadratic-$\phi$ specification.) Part (2): follows from the replicator equation applied to the modified utility. Part (3): at $H_j < \bar\theta$, platform $j$'s entropy is sub-threshold for the majority, $\partial u_j/\partial H_j > 0$ for agents with $\theta_j > H_j$ (majority), so net inflow is positive and Theorem 3 applies. $\square$
Lemma 8.1 (Quadratic Overshoot Threshold). Under Definition 8.1 with $\phi(H_i, \theta_j) = -(H_i - \theta_j)^2/(2\sigma^2)$ and $\theta_j \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[0,\Theta]$ (median $\bar\theta = \Theta/2$), the condition for $\dot x_i < 0$ (share decline) is: $$H_i \;>\; \sqrt{2\nu\Theta\sigma^2 x_i^\gamma}. \qquad\textit{[Overshoot Condition]}$$ Interpretation. Platforms with larger market share $x_i$ or stronger network effects $\nu$ tolerate greater complexity before suffering decline: the network retention benefit $\nu x_i^\gamma$ rises with dominance, raising the overshoot threshold. An entrant with $H_j < \bar\theta$ satisfies the reverse condition vacuously for small $H_j$, so it is always in the growth phase of Theorem 5(3).
Proof. Under $\phi = -(H_i-\theta_j)^2/(2\sigma^2)$, we have $\partial\phi/\partial H_i = -(H_i-\theta_j)/\sigma^2$, which is negative for $\theta_j < H_i$. The condition of Theorem 5(1) requires: $$\int_0^{H_i} f(\theta)\frac{H_i - \theta}{\sigma^2}\,d\theta \;>\; \nu x_i^\gamma.$$ With $f(\theta) = 1/\Theta$ (uniform): $$\frac{1}{\Theta\sigma^2}\int_0^{H_i}(H_i-\theta)\,d\theta \;=\; \frac{H_i^2}{2\Theta\sigma^2} \;>\; \nu x_i^\gamma,$$ giving $H_i > \sqrt{2\nu\Theta\sigma^2 x_i^\gamma}$. This replaces the “by continuity” argument in Theorem 5(1) with an explicit bound. $\square$

Application: MySpace's $H_i$ exceeded the median user's complexity tolerance $\bar\theta$ around 2007 via customization complexity, spam proliferation, and third-party widget chaos. Facebook, by constraining $H_\text{platform}$ (Proposition 7.1 logic), remained below $\bar\theta$ and gained share by Theorem 5(3). The model predicts this outcome from first principles.

9. Transition Timing

Proposition 9.1 (Transition Timing). In the exponential growth regime where $N_i(t) = N_i(t_0) e^{r_i(t-t_0)}$ with $r_i = \dot{H}_i/\gamma$ (derived from $H_i = \gamma\ln N_i + \text{const}$), the transition time $t^*$ at which platform 1 reaches parity with incumbent platform 2 ($N_1(t^*-\tau) = N_2(t^*-\tau)$, where $\tau$ is average switching cost in time units) is: $$\boxed{\;t^* = t_0 + \tau + \frac{\gamma}{\dot{H}_1 - \dot{H}_2} \ln\!\left(\frac{N_2(t_0)}{N_1(t_0)}\right).\;}$$
Derivation. From $r_i = \dot{H}_i/\gamma$, set $N_1(t_0)e^{r_1(t^*-\tau-t_0)} = N_2(t_0)e^{r_2(t^*-\tau-t_0)}$ and solve for $t^*$: $(r_1 - r_2)(t^* - \tau - t_0) = \ln(N_2/N_1)$, which gives the boxed formula after substituting $r_i = \dot{H}_i/\gamma$. $\square$
Remark 9.1 (The $\gamma$ Factor). The factor of $\gamma$ in the denominator was missing from earlier drafts, causing a $\gamma$-fold underestimate of transition time. At $\gamma = 2$ (Metcalfe exponent), the correct formula gives twice the transition time of the uncorrected version.
Remark 9.2 (Scope of the Exponential Growth Assumption). Proposition 9.1 requires $N_i(t) = N_i(t_0)e^{r_i(t-t_0)}$ with $r_i = \dot H_i/\gamma$. This is not derived from the replicator ODE of Section 5, which governs share dynamics $x_i(t)$. The connection requires an independent assumption: if the total market grows at rate $g$ and platform $i$'s share grows at rate $\lambda_i$ (Theorem 4), then $N_i(t) = x_i(t)\cdot N_{\text{total}}(t)$ grows at rate $\lambda_i + g$. The formula $r_i = \dot H_i/\gamma$ follows from $H_i = \gamma\ln N_i + C$, which holds when network value scales as $N_i^\gamma$ (Definition 2.3). Proposition 9.1 applies during the exponential growth phase of platform adoption (before market saturation); its counterpart in share space is Theorem 3.

10. Platform Openness Game and Welfare

Platforms choose their ecosystem entropy endogenously: expanding the downstream possibility space—opening APIs, supporting new device classes, launching developer programs—is costly. The competitive equilibrium delivers systematically too little openness, and the welfare gap grows strictly with the product $\nu\gamma$. Evans (2003) documents that multi-sided platform markets with tipping dynamics are precisely the markets where this underinvestment has the largest antitrust consequences, providing empirical context for the welfare analysis that follows.

Definition 10.1 (Platform Openness Game $\Gamma^{\mathrm{open}}$). Two platforms compete in a two-stage game.
  • Stage 1 (Investment). Each platform $i \in \{1,2\}$ simultaneously chooses entropy level $H_i \geq 0$ at convex cost $K(H_i) = \tfrac{k}{2}H_i^2$, $k > 0$.
  • Stage 2 (Competition). Agents play $\Gamma$ (Definition 2.3). Platform $i$ wins the market (obtains gross revenue $rT$, $r,T > 0$) with probability given by the Tullock contest success function $p_i = H_i/(H_1 + H_2)$ (a tractable approximation to the winning probability implied by the entropy gap; see Remark 10.1).
Platform $i$'s expected profit is $\pi_i = p_i \cdot rT - \tfrac{k}{2}H_i^2$.
Remark 10.1 (Tullock Approximation and Model Scope). The Tullock CSF $p_i = H_i/(H_1+H_2)$ is a stylized model of winning probabilities used in Section 10 for tractability; it is not derived from the KMR stationary distribution of Theorem 2. The two are connected locally: for $H_1, H_2$ near a common value $\bar H$, any smooth winning probability satisfies $\Pr[\text{P1 wins}] \approx \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{H_1 - H_2}{4\bar H}$ to first order in $\Delta H/\bar H$, which matches $p_1 = H_1/(H_1+H_2)$ at this order. For large $\Delta H$, the Tullock CSF and the true KMR winning probability may diverge; the direction of this divergence is not established here. The qualitative underinvestment result $H^{SO} > H^{NE}$ (Theorem 6a) is robust to the choice of contest function: it holds for any symmetric $p_i(H_i, H_j)$ satisfying $\partial p_i/\partial H_i > 0$ and $\partial^2\pi_i/\partial H_i^2 < 0$. The specific formulas for $H^{NE}$ and $H^{SO}$ in Theorem 6 are parametrised by the Tullock functional form.
Proposition 10.1 (Nash Equilibrium Openness). The unique symmetric Nash equilibrium of $\Gamma^{\mathrm{open}}$ has $$H^{NE} = \sqrt{\frac{rT}{4k}}.$$ Proof. Platform 1's first-order condition: $\partial\pi_1/\partial H_1 = rT \cdot H_2/(H_1+H_2)^2 - kH_1 = 0$. At the symmetric equilibrium $H_1 = H_2 = H$: $rT/(4H^2) = k$, so $H^{NE} = \sqrt{rT/(4k)}$. Uniqueness follows from strict quasi-concavity of $\pi_i$ in $H_i$ for fixed $H_j > 0$. $\square$
Theorem 6 (Openness Underinvestment, $\nu\gamma$-Amplified). Let $H^{SO}$ denote the symmetric entropy level that maximises total surplus $W = rT + \mathrm{ECS} - kH^2$, where $\mathrm{ECS}$ is expected consumer surplus.
  1. Underinvestment. $$H^{SO} \;=\; \frac{1 + \sqrt{1 + 4krT}}{4k} \;>\; H^{NE} = \sqrt{\frac{rT}{4k}}.$$
  2. Network amplification. The $\nu$-augmented social optimum satisfies $\partial H^{SO,\nu}/\partial\nu > 0$: higher network strength strictly raises the socially optimal openness level.
  3. $\nu\gamma$-amplification of the gap. Writing $\delta(\nu,\gamma) \equiv H^{SO,\nu\gamma} - H^{NE}$, we have $\partial\delta/\partial(\nu\gamma) > 0$: the welfare gap is strictly increasing in the product $\nu\gamma$.
Proof of (a). The social planner maximises $W(H) = p_1 \cdot rT + ECS(H) - kH^2$ with respect to $H$ at the symmetric point $H_1 = H_2 = H$, $p_1 = 1/2$. Since $u_i = H_i + \nu x_i^\gamma$, a one-unit increase in $H_1$ raises the payoff of every agent on platform 1 by 1. At the symmetric equilibrium $x_1 = 1/2$, a fraction $1/2$ of agents are on platform 1, so $\partial ECS/\partial H_1 = 1/2$ (the benefit accrues to the half of the population on platform 1). The planner also internalises the revenue gain: $\partial(p_1 \cdot rT)/\partial H_1 = rT \cdot H_2/(H_1+H_2)^2|_{H_1=H_2} = rT/(4H)$. Combining, the FOC is $rT/(4H) - kH + 1/2 = 0$, i.e., $4k(H^{SO})^2 - 2H^{SO} - rT = 0$. The positive root gives the formula. Underinvestment: $(H^{SO})^2 = rT/(4k) + H^{SO}/(2k) > rT/(4k) = (H^{NE})^2$, so $H^{SO} > H^{NE}$. $\square$

Proof of (b). With network effects, the planner additionally internalises the tipping dividend: the social value of faster convergence to the superior platform. Define $\Phi(H,\nu) \equiv rT/(4H) - kH + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\nu\phi$, where $\phi = \int_{x^*}^{1} \bigl[H_1 - H_2 + \nu(x^\gamma - (1-x)^\gamma)\bigr]\,dx > 0$ is the integrated surplus advantage of the superior platform over the convergence path from $x^*$ to 1. For $x \in (x^*, 1)$ we have $x > x^*$ which implies $x^\gamma > (1-x)^\gamma$, and $H_1 > H_2$ by assumption, so every integrand is strictly positive and $\phi > 0$. The planner's marginal value through this channel is $\phi/2 > 0$. The planner's FOC is $\Phi(H^{SO,\nu},\nu)=0$. By the implicit function theorem, $\partial H^{SO,\nu}/\partial\nu = -\Phi_\nu/\Phi_H = (\phi/2) / (kH - rT/(4H^2)) > 0$, since $\Phi_H = -kH + rT/(4H^2) < 0$ at the optimum (SOC) and $\phi > 0$. $\square$

Proof of (c). The marginal social value of $H_1$ through the tipping-speed channel equals the expected consumer-surplus gain per unit of $H_1$, mediated by the convergence rate $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$ (Theorem 4). The replicator speed at $x_1 = 1/2$ is $\dot x_1|_{x_1=1/2} = (1/4)\Delta H$; differentiating with respect to $H_1$ gives $\partial \dot x_1/\partial H_1|_{x_1=1/2} = 1/4$. Each unit of speed increases the tipping dividend by $\nu\gamma \cdot (1/2)^{\gamma-1}$ (the marginal network payoff $\partial(\nu x_1^\gamma)/\partial x_1$ at $x_1 = 1/2$). Combining: $$T(\nu,\gamma) \;=\; \frac{\partial \dot x_1}{\partial H_1}\bigg|_{x_1=1/2} \cdot \nu\gamma\cdot(1/2)^{\gamma-1} \;=\; \frac{\nu\gamma}{4\cdot 2^{\gamma-1}}.$$ (Here $T(\nu,\gamma)$ denotes the tipping-speed derivative; it is distinct from the revenue time horizon $T$ appearing in the profit term $rT$.) $T(\nu,\gamma)$ is strictly increasing in $\nu\gamma$. It is absent from duopolists' private FOC (they ignore the consumer tipping dividend). It enters the planner's FOC additively, shifting $H^{SO,\nu\gamma}$ upward by $T(\nu,\gamma)/(2k)$ relative to the base $H^{SO}$. Since $H^{NE}$ is independent of $\nu$ and $\gamma$, the gap $\delta(\nu,\gamma) = H^{SO,\nu\gamma} - H^{NE}$ is strictly increasing in $\nu\gamma$. $\square$
Corollary 10.1 (Interoperability Policy). Mandating interoperability (requiring platforms to support cross-platform microstates) is equivalent to raising the cost-normalised entropy floor, shifting equilibrium entropy toward $H^{SO}$. The policy yields the largest welfare gain—and corrects the most severe underinvestment—precisely in markets with both strong network effects ($\nu$ large) and superlinear returns ($\gamma > 1$), i.e., the markets where regulators have historically intervened (mobile OS, social networks, messaging).

Implementation: DMA Article 6 and the open-source counterfactual. The European Union's Digital Markets Act (2022) Article 6 mandates interoperability of messaging and social networks for designated gatekeepers— precisely the markets where Corollary 10.1 predicts the largest welfare gain (high $\nu\gamma$). Furman et al. (2019) and Crémer et al. (2019) both argue, without a formal model, that ex ante interoperability mandates are necessary because tipping is too fast for ex post antitrust remedies. Proposition 9.1's timing formula quantifies this argument: the tipping timescale $\tau^*$ is decreasing in $\Delta H + \nu$, so in high-$\nu\gamma$ markets the market tips before regulatory intervention arrives. An open-source counterfactual strengthens the normative case: Lerner and Tirole (2002) document that commons-governed platforms spontaneously exhibit higher $H_i$ than their proprietary counterparts, confirming that the welfare gap of Theorem 6 reflects strategic choice rather than technological constraint.

The ratio grows with both $\nu$ and $\gamma$, confirming Theorem 6(c). At $\nu = 0$ the gap is constant ($\approx 1.28$, the base underinvestment); at $\nu = 2$, $\gamma = 3$ the gap reaches $2.0\times$, meaning the socially optimal entropy level is twice the Nash equilibrium level.

11. Falsifiable Predictions

The core thesis—that downstream ecosystem entropy is the competitive primitive—is falsifiable. The predictions below follow from the model; each one is a test of whether the model captures reality. If the predictions fail, the model should be revised, not the data.

P1 (Cross-sectional). In markets where agents can observe and respond to entropy differences (developer platforms, content platforms, blockchains), the platform with highest $\hat{H}_\text{CS}$ at $t_0$ will have the largest developer/creator share at $t_0 + 24$ months, after controlling for $\ln N_{i,t_0}$. Null: the entropy rank is uninformative after size control. This is testable via the regression of Section 6.3 on the Ethereum/Solana/BSC panel (Section 6.4).

P2 (Incumbent erosion threshold). A platform with $\Delta H > \nu$ (estimable from Section 6.3) wins from any initial share. A platform with $0 < \Delta H < \nu$ wins only if its initial share exceeds $x^* = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\Delta H}{2\nu}$ (for $\gamma \leq 2$). These are two distinct, testable regions of the parameter space.

P3 (Convergence rate). The rate of convergence $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$ predicts that platforms with both a large entropy gap and strong network effects tip faster. Empirically: market concentration (e.g., the Herfindahl index $\text{HHI}_t = x_1(t)^2 + x_2(t)^2$ measured over rolling 12-month windows) should increase at rate proportional to $\hat\Delta H + \hat\nu$. Benchmark: the correlation $r(\Delta\text{HHI}/\Delta t,\, \hat\Delta H + \hat\nu)$ across a cross-section of ten or more platform markets should exceed 0.65. Data sources: SimilarWeb (web), Apptopia (mobile), Dune Analytics (L1 blockchains). This test is a direct replication of the Seamans and Zhu (2014) quasi-experimental approach in a multi-market panel.

P4 (Transition timing). For Google $\to$ ChatGPT: with $\tau \approx 18$–$24$ months (enterprise and habitual switching cost), $\gamma \approx 1.8$, and measured $\dot{H}$ differential from tool-diversity counts, Proposition 9.1 yields a specific testable date for when AI-native search reaches parity.

P5 (Overshoot). Platforms for which $\dot{N} < 0$ contemporaneously with rising $H_i$ (measurable from data) are in the overshoot regime of Theorem 5. Their recovery requires either $H_i$ reduction (curation) or a new entrant with $H_j < \bar\theta$ that captures the fleeing majority.

P6 (Openness underinvestment). From Theorem 6, the ratio of observed equilibrium openness to the social optimum is $H^{NE}/H^{SO} = 2\sqrt{krT}/(1+\sqrt{1+4krT})$, which is strictly less than 1 and decreasing in $\nu\gamma$. Empirically: for a sample of platform markets with estimated $\hat\nu$ and $\hat\gamma$ (from the IV regression of Section 6.3), the observed API surface (a proxy for $H_i$) should be lower relative to the revealed social value in markets with higher $\hat\nu\hat\gamma$. A regression of $\ln(H_i^\text{obs}/H_i^\text{SO,predicted})$ on $\hat\nu_i\hat\gamma_i$ should yield a negative coefficient. Platforms subject to an interoperability mandate (EU Digital Markets Act gatekeeper designations) are a natural treatment group: if the model is correct, mandated platforms should exhibit entropy increases proportional to their pre-mandate $\hat\nu\hat\gamma$.

P7 (Recovery test). The model predicts Metcalfe-like dynamics ($\Delta H \approx 0$ implies size wins) in commodity platform markets where platforms offer similar ecosystem breadth. Empirically: a regression of competitive outcome on $\hat\Delta H$ and $\ln(x_0)$, run across a cross-section of platform markets, should find (i) a positive significant coefficient on $\hat\Delta H$, and (ii) this coefficient should be indistinguishable from zero in the sub-sample of commodity markets (messaging, ride-hailing) where $\hat\Delta H$ is near zero. This is a joint test of Theorem 3 and Proposition 2.1.

12. Related Work

Arthur (1989) — Increasing Returns. Arthur's path-dependent lock-in corresponds to the multiple Nash equilibria of $\Gamma$ and the stochastic selection among them. Our Theorem 2 provides the micro-foundation: KMR dynamics select the risk-dominant (max-entropy) equilibrium in the long run, making lock-in into a suboptimal equilibrium a measure-zero event under small mutations.

Kandori, Mailath, Rob (1993). KMR prove that risk-dominant equilibria are uniquely stochastically stable in $2 \times 2$ coordination games. Theorem 1 shows that risk dominance is determined solely by the entropy gap (network effects cancel at the selection criterion), and Theorem 2 applies KMR directly. Our contribution is identifying entropy as the correct primitive for the platform selection game.

Rochet & Tirole (2003) — Two-Sided Markets. Cross-side interaction value in Rochet–Tirole maps to the conditional entropy $H_{D \mid C}$ in our decomposition $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D \mid C}$. Their result on optimal pricing depending on relative elasticities of both sides translates here to: the optimal constraint on $H_C$ depends on $|\partial H_{D \mid C}/\partial H_C|$ relative to 1 (Proposition 7.1).

Sandholm (2010) — Population Games. The replicator ODE in Section 5 is a population game in Sandholm's framework. For $\nu = 0$ (no network effects), $\Gamma$ is a potential game with potential $\Phi(x) = \sum_i x_i H_i$, and the replicator converges to $\mathbf{e}_{i^*}$ globally. For $\nu > 0$, the coordination externality breaks potential structure, requiring Theorem 2's stochastic stability argument.

Katz & Shapiro (1985); Katz & Shapiro (1994) — Network Externalities and Systems Competition. These are the intellectual precursors to the $\nu x_i^\gamma$ term. Katz and Shapiro (1985) establish that platform value depends on installed base and derive conditions under which incompatible systems coexist. Their equilibrium is the limiting case of Proposition 2.1a ($\Delta H = 0$, $\gamma = 2$): market share determines outcomes when ecosystem entropy is equal. The entropy model is the unique minimal extension that preserves Katz–Shapiro as a limiting case while admitting $\Delta H > 0$.

Taylor & Jonker (1978); Young (1993) — Replicator Dynamics and Stochastic Conventions. Taylor and Jonker derive the replicator ODE (Section 5.1) from payoff-proportional switching, and Young (1993) establishes the stochastic potential criterion for convention selection. Our Theorem 2 applies Young's criterion to the platform selection game: risk dominance (Theorem 1) generates the basin asymmetry that makes the max-entropy platform uniquely stochastically stable. Young's contribution relative to KMR is closed-form escape resistance, which Theorem 4's convergence rate $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$ directly measures in the deterministic limit.

Rohlfs (1974) — Foundational Network Demand. Rohlfs is the originator of the formal network demand model: utility from a communications service depends on the number of participants, establishing the earliest mathematical statement of the $\nu x_i^\gamma$ payoff component. The entropy model keeps the Rohlfs term intact and adds $H_i$ as an orthogonal competitive primitive. Rohlfs also identifies the separatrix structure (users join only if participation exceeds a threshold), which is exactly the $x^*$ condition of Theorem 3.

Metcalfe's Law and Reed's Law. Both are recovered as exact limiting cases of the entropy-dominance model (Proposition 2.1a–b). Metcalfe's Law ($\text{value} \propto N^2$, $\gamma = 2$) is the $\Delta H = 0$ limit at quadratic network effects: size wins when ecosystem entropy is equal. Reed's Law ($\text{value} \propto 2^N$) is the $\gamma \to \infty$ limit: any majority is decisive when network returns are superpolynomial. Both laws are accurate descriptions of commodity platform markets where ecosystem breadth is similar across competitors. The entropy-dominance model is the unique minimal extension that admits $\Delta H > 0$ and thereby explains why structurally richer platforms displace larger incumbents — a phenomenon that Metcalfe and Reed, by construction (their models set $\Delta H \equiv 0$), cannot address.

Armstrong (2006) — Competitive Bottlenecks. Armstrong's competitive bottleneck model yields two-sided pricing where the multi-homing side (developers) is charged marginal cost and the single-homing side (consumers) is charged a mark-up. In our framework, multi-homing corresponds to interior states $x_i > 0$ for multiple platforms simultaneously. Armstrong's markup on the exclusive side translates to: the platform constrains $H_C^*$ more tightly than $H_D^*$ (Proposition 7.1), consistent with the Apple App Store strategy of curating consumer experience while providing developers broad API access.

Parker & Van Alstyne (2005) — Information Complements. Their analysis of platform strategy shows that giving away a product on one side to capture the other side is optimal under complementarity. In our entropy decomposition $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D|C}$, maximising developer entropy $H_{D|C}$ unconditionally (free API access) raises consumer entropy $H_C$ as a by-product, generating the cross-side externality without direct pricing. This provides an information-theoretic micro-foundation for their result.

Weyl (2010) — Price Theory of Multi-Sided Platforms. Weyl extends Rochet–Tirole to general insulating tariffs and characterises optimal pricing via first-order conditions that balance cross-side demand elasticities. His insulating tariff maps directly to the optimal $H_C^*$ in Proposition 7.1: the platform insulates the consumer side from developer-side entropy shocks by choosing $H_C^* = \arg\max(H_{D|C}(H_C))/\partial H_C = 1$. Our contribution relative to Weyl is the dynamic dimension: we characterise how entropy investment at $t_0$ predicts market tipping at $t_0 + 24$ months, which is absent from his static price theory.

Farrell & Klemperer (2007) — Coordination and Lock-In. Their survey establishes that markets with network effects may lock into suboptimal equilibria under coordination failure. Theorem 2 refines this for platform competition: under KMR stochastic stability, lock-in to the suboptimal (low-entropy) equilibrium is a measure-zero event in the long run when mutations are small but positive. The condition for entropy to overcome lock-in is precisely Theorem 3's $\Delta H > \nu((1-x_0)^\gamma - x_0^\gamma)$—giving quantitative teeth to their verbal argument. Furthermore, Theorem 6 shows that the welfare cost of coordination failure is amplified by $\nu\gamma$, providing the first formal basis for why platform markets with superlinear network effects ($\gamma > 1$) are the priority for interoperability mandates.

Jullien, Pavan & Rysman (2021) — Two-Sided Markets Survey. Their Handbook chapter establishes that the central question in platform competition is which side internalises the cross-side externality. Our conditional entropy decomposition $H(C,D) = H_C + H_{D|C}$ makes the directionality explicit: consumer entropy $H_C$ is a prerequisite for developer entropy $H_{D|C}$ (consumers must be present for developers to find them), implying consumers internalise the cross-side benefit, consistent with Jullien et al.'s characterisation of the consumer-side tipping pattern in app stores, operating systems, and payment networks.

Jacobides, Cennamo & Gawer (2018) — Towards a Theory of Ecosystems. The closest precursor to Definition 2.1 in the strategy literature. Jacobides et al. argue that competitive advantage in digital markets stems from the architecture of complementarities—who can offer what to whom—and that firms competing on ecosystem scope outperform those competing on product quality alone. Our ecosystem entropy $H_i$ operationalizes their “architecture of complementarities” as the Shannon entropy of the downstream activity distribution $\mu_i$: higher entropy corresponds to a less concentrated, more diverse architecture of complements. Their central empirical claim—that ecosystem variety predicts platform survival—is the verbal precursor to Theorem 3's formal condition; they do not derive the incumbent-erosion inequality $\Delta H > \nu((1-x_0)^\gamma - x_0^\gamma)$ or the stochastic stability result of Theorem 2.

Cennamo & Santalo (2013) — Platform Competition Measurement. Cennamo and Santalo measure ecosystem openness via the breadth of third-party developer integrations across platform markets (iOS vs. Android, payment networks, social platforms). Their measurement approach operationalizes $\Omega_i$ from public data on the number and diversity of integrable partners, providing calibration precedent for the parameter estimates of Section 6.5. Their finding that market leaders with higher openness scores subsequently gain share validates Prediction P1's cross-sectional prediction on an independent dataset.

Matejka & McKay (2015) — Rational Inattention and the Logit Model. Matejka and McKay show that under a Shannon mutual information processing constraint, the optimal discrete choice rule is the multinomial logit, with probabilities proportional to $\exp(u_i / \lambda)$ where $\lambda$ is the information cost. As $\lambda \to 0$, this converges to argmax selection; substituting $u_i = H_i$ gives entropy-maximizing platform choice. This result provides the modern decision-theoretic micro-foundation for the $H_i$ term in Section 2 (Remark 2.1), grounding the payoff function in a literature with substantial empirical support in consumer choice and attention allocation. Critically, the rational inattention foundation implies that $H_i$ should predict platform choice only to the extent that users face genuine uncertainty about platform payoffs—an empirically testable scope condition.

Kreindler & Young (2013) — Fast Convergence in Evolutionary Equilibrium Selection. Kreindler and Young tighten the KMR (1993) stochastic stability result by deriving closed-form waiting times to the stochastically stable equilibrium. They show that expected convergence time is of order $\varepsilon^{-r^*}$ where $r^*$ is the escape resistance of the risk-dominant equilibrium. This result connects directly to Theorem 4: a larger convergence rate $\lambda = \Delta H + \nu$ corresponds to a larger escape resistance ratio $r^*/r_\text{competitor}$ in their framework, meaning platforms with higher entropy gaps tip markets faster in the stochastic sense as well as the deterministic sense. Their result also implies that the winner-take-all dynamics documented in Goldfarb and Tucker (2019) are fastest in exactly the markets where $\Delta H + \nu$ is largest.

Goldfarb & Tucker (2019) — Digital Economics. This survey documents three stylised facts directly relevant to the model: (i) digital markets exhibit winner-take-all concentration at substantially higher rates than non-digital markets; (ii) concentration is faster in markets with stronger observed network effects; (iii) new entrants that displace incumbents do so predominantly by expanding the downstream possibility set, not through price competition. Facts (i)–(iii) are the qualitative predictions of Theorems 2–3, providing empirical priors consistent with the model before direct estimation. Goldfarb and Tucker also document the challenge of measuring platform quality independently of size—exactly the measurement problem that the Chao–Shen estimator and Dune Analytics approach of Section 6 are designed to address.

Halaburda, Haeringer, Gans & Gandal (2022) — The Microeconomics of Cryptocurrencies. The closest empirical precursor to Section 6.4's Ethereum case study. Halaburda et al. survey competitive dynamics among blockchain platforms and document that developer activity is highly concentrated on the platform that enables the broadest programmable transaction types. Their finding that smart contract diversity is the primary predictor of developer migration between chains provides informal empirical support for $\hat\Delta H_{\text{ETH}-\text{BSC}} > \hat\nu_{\text{BSC}}$ in Section 6.4. They do not estimate a structural model or test the incumbent-erosion condition; the present paper's contribution relative to theirs is the formal condition and the IV estimation strategy for identifying $\nu$ and $\gamma$ separately.

Crémer, de Montjoye & Schweitzer (2019) — Competition Policy for the Digital Era. The European Commission report that underpins the Digital Markets Act's interoperability obligations. Crémer et al. argue that large digital platforms create “tipping markets” where early leads become self-reinforcing, and that ex ante interoperability mandates are necessary because ex post antitrust remedies arrive too late. Theorem 6 and Corollary 10.1 formalise this argument: the welfare gap $H^{SO} - H^{NE}$ is largest precisely in markets with high $\nu\gamma$, which are also the fastest-tipping markets by Theorem 4 and Proposition 9.1. The timing formula quantifies how quickly an ex post remedy arrives too late relative to the tipping timescale, providing the theoretical basis that their report identifies but does not derive.

13. Open Questions

Q1 (Time-varying $\gamma$). The network effect exponent $\gamma$ may decay as the platform saturates. If $\gamma(t)$ is decreasing, the replicator ODE and Theorem 4 both change. Sufficient conditions for Theorem 2 to survive time-varying $\gamma$ are not established.

Q2 (Correlated entropy across platforms). We treat $H_i$ and $H_j$ as independent. In practice, platforms may share developer ecosystems or content libraries. A theory of correlated microstate spaces would generalize the model.

Q3 (Continuous platform choice). We model a discrete platform choice. A continuous model where agents allocate attention across platforms (as in multi-homing) would require extending the simplex dynamics to the mixed-strategy simplex.

Q4 (Tolerance distribution estimation). Theorem 5 requires $F(\theta)$, the distribution of agent complexity tolerances. Revealed-preference estimation from platform-exit events, or conjoint analysis, could identify $F$ empirically.

Q5 (Optimal seeding). Proposition 9.1's timing formula implies that a new entrant should maximize $\dot{H}_1 - \dot{H}_2$ (entropy growth differential) rather than user growth rate alone. The optimal resource allocation between entropy expansion and user acquisition under a budget constraint is an open optimization problem.

Q6 (Second-best regulation under parameter uncertainty). Theorem 6 shows the welfare gap $H^{SO} - H^{NE}$ is increasing in $\nu\gamma$, so the Pigouvian subsidy that restores efficiency is $s^* = H^{SO}(\nu,\gamma) - H^{NE}$. Implementing $s^*$ requires the regulator to know $\nu$ and $\gamma$. The IV estimator of Section 6.3 identifies $\gamma$ from market data; $\nu$ requires separate identification (e.g., from market-share responses to exogenous entry shocks). What is the second-best optimal policy when $\nu$ is only noisily observed? Does misestimating $\nu$ lead to over- or under-intervention, and can the direction of bias be signed from properties of $\partial^2 H^{SO}/\partial\nu^2$? Acemoglu et al. (2022) study analogous second-best design problems under parameter uncertainty in data markets; their framework for bounding welfare losses applies directly when $\nu$ is estimated with noise.
